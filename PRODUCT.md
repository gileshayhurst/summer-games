# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Groups of friends who play casual games together — garage, backyard, parties, dorms, bars. Pong, Beer Die, Hearts, Cornhole, Spikeball, Pool, Poker. They are not analysts; they're players who want to know who's winning and have something to argue about.

Context of use is decisive: **games get logged on a phone, one-handed, mid-party** — often a little drunk, definitely not sitting at a desk. Reading (leaderboards, recent games, someone's profile) happens on a phone too, passed around the group. The web app is meant to be added to the home screen and used like a native app.

The job to be done: **settle who's actually winning and fuel the trash talk.** Standings, win rates, hot and cold streaks, head-to-head records — the ammunition for bragging rights.

## Product Purpose

Garage League is the shared, unofficial-official scoreboard for a friend group's games. One group has a playerbase, logs game results, and gets live leaderboards, streaks, and per-player stats across every game they play. Success looks like a group that keeps coming back to log the next game and check where they stand — the app becomes the accepted record of who won.

It is deliberately free, low-friction, and social: public shareable links, join codes, claim-your-player. The point is not precision analytics; it's a scoreboard the whole crew trusts and enjoys checking.

## Brand Personality

**Loud, sporty, playful.** Sports-broadcast energy — the app talks like a scoreboard graphic, not a spreadsheet. Three words: **punchy, competitive, tongue-in-cheek.** The tagline says it: *"The unofficial official scoreboard."*

The voice is confident and a little cheeky — it celebrates winners and roasts cold streaks (🔥 hot, 😂 cold) without being mean. Warmth comes from the garage/backyard palette (cream, amber, orange) and the human detail of real player names and rivalries, not from softness. Emotional goal: the small hit of pride when you're on top of the board, and the itch to log one more game.

Keep and *sharpen* the existing loud-sporty direction — it's the right register. Sharpen means more intentional hierarchy and rhythm, not more shouting: the all-caps-black-weight-tracked treatment is the brand, but it should be deployed with contrast (not every element at once) so the important thing on each screen actually stands out.

## Anti-references

- **Corporate SaaS analytics dashboard.** No cold gray/blue enterprise look, no sterile hero-metric card grids, no "business intelligence" sterility. This is a garage, not a boardroom.
- **Childish / over-gamified.** No cartoon mascots, confetti storms, or Duolingo-style badge overload. It's fun and competitive, not a kids' game — the humor is dry (a 😂 on a losing streak), not a theme park.
- **Generic AI-template startup look.** No purple gradients, glassmorphism, floating 3D blobs, or a tiny tracked eyebrow above every section. The loud-sporty voice is specific; don't dilute it into the default.
- **Gambling/sportsbook slickness.** Even for Poker — keep the friendly-ledger feel, not a neon betting board.

## Design Principles

1. **Scoreboard, not spreadsheet.** Every screen should read like a broadcast graphic — the winner, the streak, the standing is the hero. Data serves the bragging, so make the punchline (who's up, who's cold) unmissable, and let the supporting numbers recede.
2. **Loud with hierarchy.** The uppercase-black-tracked treatment is the brand, but loudness only works when one thing is loudest. Deploy contrast deliberately; don't let every element shout at the same volume.
3. **Thumb-first.** It's used one-handed at a party. Touch targets, primary actions, and the log flow live within thumb reach; nothing important hides behind a hover or a desktop-only layout.
4. **Roast gently, celebrate loudly.** Lean into the competitive humor — streaks, head-to-head, rivalries — but keep it warm. Never punch down; the app teases, it doesn't shame.
5. **Trustworthy record.** For the group to argue from it, the scoreboard has to feel authoritative and current. Stats read as correct and up-to-date; the fun rests on numbers people believe.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG AA.** Body text ≥4.5:1 against its (warm, tinted) background — watch the muted-gray-on-cream trap; large/bold text ≥3:1. Visible keyboard focus states on all interactive elements. Full `prefers-reduced-motion` alternatives for any streak/celebration motion. Don't rely on color alone to signal win/loss or hot/cold — pair with text, icon, or shape (the 🔥/😂 + W/L labels already help). Given the mobile-at-a-party context, ensure adequate touch-target sizing and one-handed reach even though AA is the formal bar.
